Consensus sketch

Many control planes need a single ordered story of configuration or commands across failures. Consensus protocols (Raft is the common teaching vehicle; Paxos is the classic reference) elect a leader, replicate a log, and commit entries so a majority has agreed before you act on critical transitions.

Why it costs round-trips

Safety comes from quorum intersection between successive leader eras and log positions. That intersection logic is what blocks arbitrary duplication of committed decisions—at the price of latency, careful disk sync policies, and operational tooling for membership changes.

Same story, three lenses

Partition (who can talk to whom) is not the same object as quorum (how many votes the protocol requires on the full membership). The first explorer sketch separates partition severity from the quorum-threshold slider on purpose; sketch 2 is ballot/term only; sketch 3 is append and commit. Election picks who may propose for a term; replication decides which log prefix is durable before you treat it as committed.
