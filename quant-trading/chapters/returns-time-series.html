Returns & time series

Before you optimize entries and exits, align on how returns are defined, what “i.i.d.” pretends to ignore, and which stylized facts (Campbell, Lo, MacKinlay; Cont) motivate richer models than coin-flip metaphors.

Simple vs log returns
  • Simple return — (Pt − Pt−1) / Pt−1. Compounding multiplies (1+r) terms; not additive across time in levels.
  • Log return — ln(Pt) − ln(Pt−1). Over short horizons, log ≈ simple. Intuition: log returns add across consecutive periods if you are willing to treat them as additive (still not i.i.d.).

Schematic: annualizing by scaling √h assumes variance scales linearly with horizon—convenient for back-of-envelope risk, wrong when volatility clusters or jumps.

Stylized facts (qualitative)

Empirical return series often show: no exploitable linear autocorrelation in many liquid prices (at least after microstructure adjustment), volatility clustering (large moves bunch), fat tails vs Gaussian, and leverage effects (volatility reacts asymmetrically to down moves in equities). Treat these as reasons to be humble about Gaussian backtests—not as timing rules.

Fact Research takeaway
Volatility clustering Conditional heteroskedasticity (ARCH/GARCH family) models time-varying variance; constant-σ backtests misstate tail risk.
Fat tails VaR from Normal underestimates joint crash risk; stress scenarios and tail dependence still matter.
Microstructure noise Ultra-high-frequency returns mix signal and bid–ask bounce; aggregate to a horizon where noise is tolerable for your question.

Stationarity wishful thinking

Most strategy research assumes a slowly evolving data-generating process. Structural breaks (regulation, venue rules, macro) violate strict stationarity. Typical in practice: rolling windows, regime indicators, and out-of-sample protocols—see Backtest hygiene.
