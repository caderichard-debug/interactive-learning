Costs & capacity

Paper P&L ignores friction. Typical in practice: commissions, bid–ask spread (often proxied as half-spread per crossing), borrow for shorts, taxes, and market impact—your order moving the price against you—scale with size and turnover.

Cost stack (first-pass model)
Layer What it captures Blind spot
Explicit fees Brokerage, exchange, regulatory, borrow fees where applicable. Fee tiers change with volume; read the schedule.
Spread Crossing the book pays half-spread (long) or earns it (short) depending on aggressor side—sign matters. Spread widens in stress; static bps assumptions lie.
Impact Temporary + permanent price effect of your flow vs available liquidity. Linear impact laws are schematics; real order books are discrete events.

Volatility drag (reminder)

For multiplicative compounding, large variance lowers the geometric growth rate even when the arithmetic mean is positive. Intuition: a −50% then +50% round trip loses 25% of capital; costs add insult to injury. Pair this mental model with Returns & series.

Capacity curve (qualitative)

As AUM rises, the same signal may consume more liquidity per unit time. The “capacity” where edge disappears is not a constant—it moves with volatility and competitor flow. Typical in practice: simulate participation rate caps and venue-specific constraints before marketing capacity to LPs.

Actionable heuristic

Before scaling leverage, stress-test net returns after conservative cost and half-spread assumptions. If your purported edge lives entirely inside one round-trip of the bid–ask, it is not edge for your trade size and frequency.
